Chapter 36

Chinese Original

将欲歙之，必故张之；

将欲弱之，必故强之；

将欲废之，必故兴之；

将欲取之，必故与之。

是谓微明。

柔弱胜刚强。

鱼不可脱于渊，国之利器不可以示人。

English Translation

If you want to shrink something, you must first expand it;

If you want to weaken something, you must first strengthen it;

If you want to abolish something, you must first establish it;

If you want to take something, you must first give to it.

This is called subtle wisdom.

The soft and weak overcome the hard and strong.

Fish cannot survive away from deep water; a nation's sharp weapons should not be displayed to others.

Commentary

This chapter reveals the cyclical nature of change and the power of strategic thinking. Laozi teaches that apparent opposites are interconnected—to achieve one state, one must first understand and work with its opposite. The principle that "the soft overcomes the hard" suggests that flexibility and yielding can be more effective than rigid force. The final metaphors warn against revealing one's true capabilities prematurely.

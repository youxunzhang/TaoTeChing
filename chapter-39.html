Chapter 39

Chinese Original

昔之得一者：天得一以清；地得一以宁；神得一以灵；谷得一以盈；万物得一以生；侯王得一以为天下正。

其致之也，谓天无以清，将恐裂；地无以宁，将恐废；神无以灵，将恐歇；谷无以盈，将恐竭；万物无以生，将恐灭；侯王无以正，将恐蹶。

故贵以贱为本，高以下为基。

是以侯王自称孤、寡、不谷。此非以贱为本邪？

故致誉无誉，不欲琭琭如玉，珞珞如石。

English Translation

In ancient times, those who achieved unity: Heaven achieved unity and became clear; Earth achieved unity and became stable; spirits achieved unity and became divine; valleys achieved unity and became full; all things achieved unity and came to life; rulers achieved unity and brought order to the world.

Without this unity: Heaven would crack without clarity; Earth would collapse without stability; spirits would fade without divinity; valleys would dry up without fullness; all things would perish without life; rulers would fall without bringing order.

Therefore, the noble takes the humble as its root; the high takes the low as its foundation.

Thus rulers call themselves "orphaned," "widowed," and "worthless." Is this not taking the humble as the root?

Therefore, the highest honor is to have no honor. Do not desire to be precious like jade, but common like stone.

Commentary

This chapter emphasizes the importance of unity and humility in maintaining harmony. Everything in existence—from heaven and earth to rulers and common people—depends on achieving unity with the Tao. The paradox is that true greatness comes from embracing lowliness and humility. Wise rulers understand this principle and deliberately adopt humble titles, recognizing that their authority ultimately rests on serving others rather than elevating themselves.

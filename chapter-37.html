Chapter 37

Chinese Original

道常无为而无不为。

侯王若能守之，万物将自化。

化而欲作，吾将镇之以无名之朴。

镇之以无名之朴，夫亦将不欲。

不欲以静，天下将自正。

English Translation

The Tao is always non-acting, yet nothing is left undone.

If rulers could hold to this, all things would transform naturally.

If, in transforming, desires arise, I would calm them with the nameless simplicity.

Calmed by nameless simplicity, they would become free from desire.

Without desire, there is tranquility, and the world naturally finds its proper order.

Commentary

This chapter concludes the first part of the Tao Te Ching with the concept of wu wei—effortless action. The Tao accomplishes everything by doing nothing, suggesting that the most effective leadership involves creating conditions for natural development rather than forcing outcomes. When desires arise during transformation, they should be calmed through returning to simplicity. True peace comes not from satisfying desires but from transcending them.

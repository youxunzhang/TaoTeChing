Chapter 32

Chinese Original

道常无名，朴虽小，天下莫能臣。

侯王若能守之，万物将自宾。

天地相合，以降甘露，民莫之令而自均。

始制有名，名亦既有，夫亦将知止，知止可以不殆。

譬道之在天下，犹川谷之于江海。

English Translation

The Tao is eternally nameless, though simple and small, none in the world can master it.

If rulers could hold to it, all things would naturally submit.

When heaven and earth unite, sweet dew falls, and people naturally achieve balance without being commanded.

When systems are established, names arise. Once names exist, one should know when to stop; knowing when to stop prevents danger.

The Tao's presence in the world is like streams and valleys flowing to rivers and seas.

Commentary

This chapter explores the paradox of the Tao's simplicity and power. Though the Tao appears small and humble, it is the source of all authority and order. Laozi suggests that true leadership comes from aligning with the Tao rather than imposing artificial control. The natural harmony that emerges when heaven and earth unite serves as a metaphor for the spontaneous order that arises when human society aligns with the Way.

Chapter 41

Chinese Original

上士闻道，勤而行之；中士闻道，若存若亡；下士闻道，大笑之。不笑不足以为道。

故建言有之：明道若昧，进道若退，夷道若颣；

上德若谷，广德若不足，建德若偷；质真若渝，大白若辱，大方无隅；

大器晚成，大音希声，大象无形，道隐无名。

夫唯道，善贷且成。

English Translation

When superior people hear of the Tao, they diligently practice it. When average people hear of the Tao, they sometimes keep it and sometimes lose it. When inferior people hear of the Tao, they laugh loudly at it. If they did not laugh, it would not be the Tao.

Therefore the established sayings have it: The bright Tao seems dim; the advancing Tao seems to retreat; the smooth Tao seems rough.

Highest virtue seems like a valley; great virtue seems insufficient; established virtue seems stolen; solid truth seems changeable; great purity seems sullied; great square has no corners.

Great vessels take long to complete; great music has rare sound; great form has no shape; the Tao is hidden and nameless.

Only the Tao excels at giving and completing.

Commentary

This chapter explores how different levels of understanding respond to the Tao's teachings. The paradoxes presented—bright appearing dim, advancement seeming like retreat—illustrate why the Tao often appears contradictory to conventional thinking. True wisdom, virtue, and achievement often manifest in ways opposite to what we expect. The greatest accomplishments take time, the most profound truths are simple, and the most powerful force is invisible and unnamed.
